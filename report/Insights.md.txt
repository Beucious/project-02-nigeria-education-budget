# Nigeria Education Sector Spending: Key Findings & Recommendations
### By Winning David | May 2026

---

## Finding 1: Nigeria Has Never Met the UNESCO Education Funding Benchmark

**What the data shows:** Between 2015 and 2025 Nigeria allocated an
average of 6.57% of its federal budget to education, less than half
of UNESCO's recommended 15-20% minimum threshold. 2023 recorded the
worst funding gap at 9.79 percentage points below the benchmark.

**Why it matters:** Chronic underfunding directly correlates with poor
learning outcomes, high dropout rates, and inadequate school
infrastructure across all 36 states.

**Recommendation:** The federal government should legislate a minimum
education budget floor of 15% backed by independent monitoring,
similar to the approach used in Ghana and Rwanda which have shown
measurable improvements in literacy rates after committing to
UNESCO benchmarks.

---

## Finding 2: Nominal Growth Masks Declining Priority

**What the data shows:** Nigeria's education budget grew by 421% in
naira terms from 483.2 billion naira in 2015 to 2.52 trillion naira
in 2025. However the percentage of the total federal budget allocated
to education fell from 10.75% in 2015 to 5.47% in 2025, a 49%
decline in relative priority over the same period.

**Why it matters:** Naira figures are misleading due to inflation and
currency devaluation. The real measure of government commitment is
budget share not nominal figures. Nigeria is spending more money but
treating education as a lower priority every year.

**Recommendation:** Budget reporting should be standardized to show
education spending as a percentage of total budget and GDP, not
just nominal naira figures, to give citizens and policymakers an
accurate picture of funding trends.

---

## Finding 3: Political Cycle Drives Budget Decisions

**What the data shows:** Year on year growth analysis reveals 4 years
of negative growth in 2016, 2017, 2019, and 2020, with the largest
single year increase of 58.49% occurring in 2025 under the Tinubu
administration. Budget cuts consistently follow election years.

**Why it matters:** Education funding driven by political cycles rather
than long term planning creates instability in school funding, teacher
hiring, and infrastructure development.

**Recommendation:** Nigeria should establish a dedicated Education
Stabilization Fund similar to sovereign wealth models, where a fixed
percentage of oil revenue is ring-fenced for education spending
regardless of annual budget pressures.

## Methodology

Federal budget allocation figures sourced from Federal Budget Office
Nigeria via dataphyteng. World Bank education expenditure indicators
SE.XPD.TOTL.GB.ZS and SE.XPD.TOTL.GD.ZS were used. Data covers
2015 to 2025 with World Bank figures available to 2022. SQL queries
were written in SQLite via DB Browser for SQLite. Visualization was
built in Tableau Public Desktop Edition. All data and SQL queries
are available in the GitHub repository.
